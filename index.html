Dear President Englert,

Today I am writing to you as a Temple Alumni, a fellow Owl, and a fellow privileged white male.
It was May 11th, 2019, when I sat in one of the rows of cherry chairs lined up for the 154 Commencement of Temple University. Together with over 10,000 other graduates of the class of 2019, I walked into the Liacouras Center, its stands filled with all our loved ones. The air filled with an electrifying sense of excitement, relief, and pride. After four transforming years in the heart of Philadelphia, I sat under the massive banners honoring the great teams and players of our University's 123-year-old basketball program. Moved and emotional did I reflect on my time at Temple and listened to the incredibly powerful words of student speaker Vanessa Gabrielle Chandler. Electrified by her speech, “[our] class of owls [was] spreading our wings, taking flight and taking this world by storm.” Wearing the Temple T on my gown I received my diploma and for the rest of my life became a Temple alumnus. God, did I wear that letter proudly that day.

That was 394 days ago. Yesterday, on my morning run I put on, as always, a grey long sleeve with the big white Temple T. Today, I did not. Let me tell you why.

When I read the tweets of the University’s official twitter account yesterday morning, I was confused at first. Why did Temple find it necessary to state that we “respect the first amendment rights of all, including these that express unpopular and hateful speech.” Why did the account then have to seemingly reassure us that we “do not tolerate racism within the community”? I did some research and educated myself on the issue. As I found out more about what was happening, that some fellow owls used racist slurs, my confusion turned into anger and alienation first. I would even say that for those first moments, I felt ashamed for being a Temple Owl. However, in the classrooms of Gladfelter and Fox, I learned to use my voice, to call out and address the issues that I see. Freshman year, my HR professor made us sign every test we handed in. Putting our signature on that sheet of paper and associating it with our name made us realize that this test was important. That the words on it mattered. It should ensure that we put thought and effort into our responses. My name will forever be associated with Temple University. And those tweets lacked both the thought and empathy that I expect from myself and the organizations I am associated with.

emple prides itself on being committed to building a diverse educational community, appreciation of others, open-mindedness, and respect. It claims that its policies, practices, and programs exemplify our commitment. Publicly reinforcing that "Temple respects the first amendment rights of all, including those that express unpopular and even hateful speech that is antithetical to the university's mission” is contrary to all these claims. Do not get me wrong, freedom of speech is one of the highest goods in a democracy and we should always make it a priority to protect it. However, the freedom to voice your views freely is not equal to the freedom to voice your views free of all consequences. As an institution that prides itself as being diverse and committed to providing an environment of inclusion, you have to actively take a stand against racism. There is no middle ground when it comes to racism. Here are some powerful words by Ibram Kendi that describe the issue perfectly:

“What’s the problem with being “not racist”? It is a claim that signifies neutrality: “I am not a racist, but neither am I aggressively against racism.” But there is no neutrality in the racism struggle. The opposite of “racist” isn’t “not racist.” It is “antiracist.”

Ibram X. Kendi, How to Be an Antiracist

By taking the time to highlight freedom of speech Temple seems to position itself as not-racist, instead of anti-racist. Then, following up this tweet by stating that "recognizing everyone’s First Amendment rights does not mean that Temple tolerates racism", seemingly lecturing outraged students, alumni, and antiracist owls in a tweet of 265 characters is not a worthy response.

Respect for others requires a complicated calculation in which one party agrees to moderate their own desires to consider the long-term consequences of their own behavior, to think about something other than the thing right in front of them.

Malcolm Gladwell, Talking to Strangers

If I imagine the disappointment and betrayal I felt as a privileged white male alumnus I cannot imagine how our students and alumni of color, our students, and alumni that are gay or transsexual have felt. We proudly claim to be a safe space and place of inclusion. A place where people come together and minorities are celebrated in their diversity. How can we say that when you fail to stand up for them when they need it most.

Now it is our time to be The Who that makes the positive difference we want to see in this world.

Vanessa Gabrielle Chandler, Student Spekaer at Temple's 154 Commencement

Now it is the time to speak up and make known that we are anti-racists, as individuals, as institutions, as students, as professors, as deans, as presidents, as Owls. You need to address this issue appropriately and sincerely, not a 265 character long tweet. We all deserve better.

Perseverance conquers,

Robin Goetz, Temple Class of 2019. Antiracist.
